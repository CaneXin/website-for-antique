Antique Profile
 Welcome to Antique: The Land of the Hantík
Antique (pronounced än-ti-ké / [ɐnˈtike]) is a captivating coastal province nestled in the western section of Panay Island within the Western Visayas region. Facing the vast Sulu Sea to the west, it shares its eastern borders with the provinces of Aklan, Capiz, and Iloilo. Today, the vibrant coastal town of San Jose de Buenavista serves as both its provincial capital and its most populous center.

History
Roots of the Name: From Giant Ants to Spanish Chroniclers
Long before Spanish colonizers set foot on the Philippine archipelago, Antique was already thriving as one of the three historic sakups (independent districts) of Panay.
* The Original Name: The province was natively known as Hantík, named after the large, fierce black ants that populated the island.
* The Spanish Evolution: When Spanish chroniclers arrived, influenced by French orthography, they recorded the name as Hantique (retaining a silent 'h'). This spelling was initially used for the areas surrounding the Malandog River in what is now the municipality of Hamtic.
* The Modern Shift: Hamtic originally served as the province's earliest capital before the seat of power shifted to Bugasong, and eventually settled in San Jose de Buenavista. Over time, the silent 'h' was permanently dropped, giving rise to the modern spelling and its distinct Kinaray-a pronunciation: Antique.


Population
Population & Growth Trends
The population of Antique has officially reached 643,173 as of the July 1, 2024 Census of Population (POPCEN). This updated count reflects steady development, marking an increase of 30,199 residents since 2020.
Historically, Antique’s population has grown from 134,166 in 1903 to its current 2024 figures—a testament to more than a century of continuous provincial community growth.
Population Milestone Comparisons
* Compared to 2020: Increased by 30,199 (from 612,974)
* Compared to 2015: Increased by 61,161 (from 582,012)
* Compared to 2010: Increased by 97,142
Accelerating Growth Rates
Antique’s population growth is picking up speed. Between 2020 and 2024, the province grew at an average annual rate of 1.16%. This outpaces previous intervals:
* 2020–2024: 1.16% annual growth rate (Current)
* 2015–2024: 1.13% annual growth rate
* 2015–2020: 1.10% annual growth rate


Municipalities
The 18 Municipalities of Antique

In Antique, a barangay is more than a geographical boundary—it’s an extended family. With many villages averaging just 100 closely-related families (around 600 individuals), the province thrives on an immediate, deeply ingrained system of mutual aid and kinship

Southern Antique
These towns form the historical and political heart of the province, serving as the gateway when traveling from Iloilo.
* Anini-y – The southernmost tip, famous for marine sanctuaries and heritage sites.
* Tobias Fornier (Dao) – A scenic coastal town rich in agricultural valleys.
* Hamtic – The ancient settlement cradle where the name Hantík originated.
* San Jose de Buenavista – The bustling provincial capital, economic hub, and most populous town.
* Sibalom – A massive inland municipality known for its vast mineral wealth, gemstones, and natural parks.
* Belison – The smallest municipality in terms of land area and population, known for its coastal beauty.
* San Remigio – A mountainous, landlocked paradise often called the "Little Baguio" of Antique.
Central Antique
The central strip connects the lowlands to the rugged interiors and features prominent rivers and coastal trading stops.
* Patnongon – A coastal municipality with a rich industry in clay pottery and deep-sea fishing.
* Bugasong – The historic former capital and home of the traditional Patadyong weaving heritage.
* Valderrama – A landlocked, mountainous town nested deep within the Central Panay Mountain Range.
* Laua-an – Celebrated as the traditional "Muscovado Sugar Capital" of the province.
* Barbaza – A town beautifully sandwiched between rolling mountain trails and rich coastal waters.
Northern Antique
This northern region is the epicenter for eco-tourism and adventures, serving as the bridge to Aklan (and Boracay).
* Tibiao – The adventure capital, globally recognized for its white-water kayaking, eco-treks, and Kawa hot baths.
* Culasi – Home to the majestic Mt. Madja-as and the pristine, hook-shaped sandbar of Malalison Island.
* Sebaste – A coastal town widely visited for its miraculous St. Blaise shrine and crystal-clear springs.
* Pandan – Famous for the Bugang River, award-winningly recognized as one of the cleanest inland bodies of water in the country.
* Libertad – The northernmost gateway, prominent for its sprawling Bariw mat weaving traditions.
* Caluya – An isolated island municipality in the north-western sea, famous for its rich seaweed farming, rare Tatus (coconut crabs), and major energy industries.

Languanges
Languages of Antique
While English and Tagalog are universally understood and used in government, media, and education, Antique is incredibly rich in regional languages. The native languages spoken across the province reflect its unique geography—nestled between the mountains, neighboring provinces, and isolated islands.
1. Kinaray-a (The Heart of Antique)
* The Dominant Tongue: Kinaray-a is the primary native language of the vast majority of Antiqueños. It is a distinct Austronesian language characterized by its expressive vocabulary and its unique, rolling "r" sounds.
* Cultural Significance: It serves as the soul of local literature, contemporary poetry, and traditional Sugidanon (epic chants). It is spoken in varying dialects from the southern boundaries to the northern coast.
2. Caluyanon (The Island Dialect)
* Where it is spoken: This is a localized language exclusive to the island municipality of Caluya in northern Antique.
* Characteristics: Due to Caluya's geographic isolation in the Tablas Strait, Caluyanon has developed into a distinct blend, heavily influenced by Kinaray-a, Romblomanon, and Hiligaynon.
3. Hiligaynon (Ilonggo)
* Where it is spoken: Widely spoken in the southernmost towns bordering Iloilo, as well as the provincial capital, San Jose de Buenavista, due to regional trade and commerce.
* Role: It functions as a major secondary regional language, especially for business interactions with neighboring Iloilo.
4. Cuyonon
* Where it is spoken: Predominantly spoken in the island communities and coastal strips facing the Sulu Sea.
* Connection: This language originates from the nearby Cuyo Islands of Palawan, brought over by centuries of maritime trade, fishing migrations, and intermarriages.
5. Other Border & Indigenous Languages
* Aklanon: Frequently spoken in the northernmost municipalities like Libertad and Pandan due to their close proximity and daily trade with the province of Aklan.
* Ati (Inati): The endangered, indigenous language spoken by the tribal Ati (Negrito) communities residing in the mountainous interiors of the province

Economy


Agriculture
The Economy of Antique
Antique’s economic landscape is a unique blend of abundant natural resources, thriving coastal livelihoods, and rich mineral wealth. Driven by its distinct geography, the province is powered by agriculture, fisheries, mining, and emerging eco-tourism.
1. Agriculture: The Food Basket
Antique successfully produces enough staple crops to maintain self-sufficiency, often generating a significant surplus to feed its population and trade with neighboring provinces.
* Palay (Rice): Rice is the primary agricultural crop of the province. Cultivation spans tens of thousands of hectares across irrigated lowlands, rain-fed farms, and upland areas, with irrigated fields yielding the highest output per hectare.
  

https://files01.pna.gov.ph/ograph/2021/11/11/ilo-antique-saad-project-11-11-21.jpg








* Copra & Coconut Farming: Copra stands as the province's second major agricultural commodity. Coconut plantations cover more than a third of Antique's total land area. The island municipality of Caluya and the northern town of Pandan serve as the heart of this industry, collectively producing over half of the province's total copra output.

Fishery
2. Fishery: Lifeline of the Coast
With a sprawling 296.8-kilometer coastline facing rich marine environments, fishing is the primary economic driver for Antique's 15 coastal municipalities.
* Prime Fishing Grounds: Local fishermen benefit from the rich waters of the Cuyo East Pass, the Sulu Sea, and abundant municipal waters.
* Socio-Economic Impact: The fishing season thrives year-round, peaking from December to June. Tens of thousands of households rely directly and indirectly on the fishing industry for their livelihoods.

Livestock and poultry
3. Livestock and Poultry
Livestock and poultry raising operate through a balance of small-scale backyard setups and commercial systems. Consistent growth in this sector is heavily propelled by a steady expansion in domestic poultry production.

Forestry
Forestry & Biodiversity
Antique's forests offer an undetermined abundance of raw materials, yet conservation remains a top priority.
* Protected vs. Production Forests: Nearly 60% of Antique's forestlands are classified as protected conservation zones to safeguard its biodiversity. The remaining areas are designated as production forests.
* Forest Products: The woodland areas supply raw materials like bamboo, buri, bariw, nito, abaca, and charcoal, which feed the local construction, furniture, and handicraft industries.
* Ecological Pride: The mountains of Antique are home to the Rafflesia—one of the world's largest flowers—which proudly serves as the official provincial flower. The dense vegetative covers are highest in Culasi, San Remigio, and Valderrama.

Trade, commerce, and industry
5. Trade, Commerce, and Industry
Antique maintains a steady flow of trade, exporting local raw goods and importing commercial necessities.
* Key Exports: Raw palay, processed rice, copra, traditional muscovado sugar, legumes, fresh seafood, seaweed, and hand-crafted furniture/toys.
* Key Imports: Inbound trade consists mostly of modern construction materials, dry goods, groceries, and agricultural fertilizers.
* Economic Hubs:
   * San Jose de Buenavista (Capital) acts as the primary economic and business center of the province.
   * Culasi serves as the major commercial hub for the northern municipalities.
   * Other high-potential economic towns include Sibalom, Caluya, Pandan, Hamtic, Tibiao, Bugasong, and Patnongon.
* Connectivity: The revival of commercial flights at the Antique Airport (Evelio Javier Airport) in San Jose bridges the province to major cities, accelerating both corporate commerce and the local tourism industry.

Food


Cusine & Delicates
Binabak
A true Antiqueño specialty, Binabak is a savory shrimp delicacy featuring crushed river shrimp, locally celebrated as patuyaw. The pounded shrimp is mixed with tender, young coconut meat and aromatic ginger, wrapped securely in fresh banana leaves, and steamed to perfection.
  
https://encrypted-tbn0.gstatic.com/images?q=tbn:ANd9GcQEDR2lJPt4ApvkmHIIbqQg-icZ5mBoBHmYHw&s
Ginat-an nga Dagmay
Ginat-an nga Dagmay is a rich, deeply comforting dish that showcases local, earthy flavors. It brings together dagmay (taro) leaves, stalks, and tubers, alongside hearty kadyos (pigeon peas) and savory bagongon (river snails), all slow-simmered in creamy coconut milk. For the ultimate local dining experience, pair it with steaming hot rice, fried fish, or crispy pinakas / lamayu (sun-dried fish).
  

https://encrypted-tbn0.gstatic.com/images?q=tbn:ANd9GcSethwZ15ztvC0wMmvNZ3Vz5bqIB5KxWXngcg&s




Linapwahan
A staple in Antiqueño households, Linapwahan is a wholesome, slightly sweet vegetable broth packed with fresh, local produce. It features a healthy medley of vegetables like malunggay, young papaya, saluyot, okra, alugbati, squash, string beans, ampalaya, eggplant, and kulitis. This vibrant mix is boiled together with ripe tomatoes and onions, often elevated with fresh seafood—such as freshwater shrimp, river crab, dried anchovies, or pinakas (dried fish). A touch of ginamus (shrimp paste or fish sauce) or local sea salt ties the comforting broth together.

SWEETS

Bandi
A sweet treat deeply loved by locals, Bandi is Antique’s signature peanut brittle. The traditional process begins by boiling pure, local muscovado sugar down to a rich, molten consistency. Once it hits its boiling point, a generous amount of crisp peanuts are folded into the syrup. The mixture is quickly spread onto a flat surface to cool, hardening into a crunchy, caramelized delicacy that offers a perfect blend of sweet and nutty flavors.


  

https://encrypted-tbn0.gstatic.com/images?q=tbn:ANd9GcS3XpznvJco4doUnHNtSgQljeQza1q9BM67fw&s


Butong-butong Nipa
Butong-butong Nipa is a delightfully soft, chewy, and elastic candy stick that serves as Antique’s unique version of the classic Tira-tira. This pulled candy is crafted by reducing sugarcane molasses until thick, infusing it with a bright splash of fresh calamansi juice, and finishing it with a generous sprinkle of toasted sesame seeds before it is skillfully stretched and shaped.

Kalamay-hati
A beloved native delicacy widely found across Antique, Kalamay-hati is a sweet, sticky treat that showcases classic Filipino flavors. It is made by slow-cooking a rich mixture of pure coconut milk, dark brown sugar, and finely ground sticky rice (malagkit). The result is a glossy, dense, and comforting rice cake that is often served on banana leaves.
  

https://encrypted-tbn0.gstatic.com/images?q=tbn:ANd9GcQShmP5YWUxtnvU-CD1uPZ6tVBW1LjM9FXCZQ&s




Ibos
A quintessential Filipino rice cake, Ibos is a comforting local delicacy made from pilit (glutinous or sticky rice). The rice mixture is lightly seasoned and packed into a uniquely hand-woven container made from young coconut leaves (janisa or palaspas). Once tightly sealed, these distinct golden tubes are boiled until the rice is perfectly tender and sticky. Unwrapping an Ibos reveals a fragrant, subtly flavored rice cake that is best enjoyed dipped in local muscovado sugar, paired with fresh mangoes, or served alongside hot chocolate.

The festival serves as thanksgiving and a vibrant showcase of Antiqueño traditions and community pride.

The Madja-as Festival is one of the most important cultural celebrations in Culasi, Antique. It is held as both a foundation anniversary celebration and a tribute to the legendary Mount Madja-as, the highest peak in Panay Island. The festival also reflects the mythological and historical identity of the province, inspired by the ancient Madja-as Confederation, believed to be part of early Panay civilization.


Historical and Cultural Meaning
The festival commemorates:
The founding of Culasi in 1735
The rich history of early settlements in Antique
The cultural identity of Antiqueños rooted in Panay heritage
The significance of Mount Madja-as, considered sacred in local folklore

SAN JUAN BAUTISTA PARISH CHURCH
  



Patnongon Church, known locally as San Juan Bautista Parish Church, is a significant historical and cultural landmark located in the town of Patnongon, Antique, Philippines. This church is notable for its beautiful architecture and deep-rooted religious significance within the community. Built during the Spanish colonial period, it showcases a blend of Gothic and Baroque architectural styles that reflect the rich history of the Philippines.


The structure is constructed using local materials, contributing to its unique charm and authenticity. Visitors are often captivated by its intricate details, including the stunning altar and the beautiful stained-glass windows that depict various biblical scenes. The church serves not only as a place of worship but also as a community gathering space, where locals celebrate various religious festivals and events.


Patnongon Church is famous for its historical significance and architectural beauty. It stands as a testament to the Spanish influence in the Philippines and serves as a focal point for the town's cultural heritage. The church is also known for its vibrant community celebrations, particularly during religious festivals, which attract both locals and tourists alike.
The history of Patnongon Church dates back to the Spanish colonial era in the 19th century when it was established to serve the growing Christian community in the area. The church has witnessed numerous historical events and changes over the years, including the impact of World War II and the growth of the town itself. Its preservation reflects the community's dedication to maintaining their cultural and historical identity.


The best time to visit Patnongon Church is during the dry season, from November to April, when the weather is more favorable for sightseeing. Additionally, visiting during religious festivals, particularly the feast day of San Juan Bautista, offers a unique opportunity to experience the vibrant local culture and traditions.
